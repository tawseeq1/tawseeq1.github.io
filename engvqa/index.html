Overview

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) do well on general multimodal benchmarks, but their ability to perform engineering reasoning is largely unexplored. Engineering problems are different: they require reading technical diagrams, choosing the right governing physical principles, and staying physically consistent across many reasoning steps — where a wrong intermediate step can produce a superficially plausible but physically invalid answer. Existing benchmarks mostly grade the final answer, so these intermediate failures go unnoticed.

We introduce EngVQA, a multimodal benchmark of 696 authentic engineering problems across five subjects, and EngJudge, an 8-stage, dependency-aware evaluation framework that grades how a solution was reached rather than just the final number. Benchmarking SOTA open and closed VLMs reveals substantial weaknesses — especially in diagram interpretation, equation selection, and physically consistent multi-step analysis. Human validation confirms the framework aligns closely with expert judgment.

Key Contributions

EngVQA benchmark

696 authentic problems requiring joint reasoning over technical diagrams, physical principles, symbolic derivations, and multi-step quantitative analysis.

EngJudge framework

An 8-stage process-oriented evaluator that models dependency-aware reasoning failures and agrees strongly with expert human graders.

Diagnostic findings

SOTA VLMs reason well on setup but collapse during execution — no model exceeds a mean score of 4 in any subject.

EngVQA spans Fluid Mechanics, Heat & Mass Transfer, Dynamics, Mechanics of Materials, and Thermodynamics. Problems are drawn from standard graduate-level engineering textbooks and course problem sets. A semi-automated pipeline (Adobe PDF Extract, PyMuPDF, and a Gemini model) recovers layouts, figures, equations, and symbolic notation, and every question, diagram, and solution is manually verified against the reference material. Solving a typical problem requires integrating several physical concepts at once, making the benchmark far harder than single-template datasets.

How EngJudge Works

Each solution is generated in a fixed tagged format and split into eight reasoning stages: Problem Characterization, Assumptions, Visual Interpretation, Equation Selection, Logical Reasoning, Algebraic Accuracy, Physical Interpretation, and Final Answer. Every stage starts at 10 and loses points for detected errors (a four-level severity scale of 2 / 4 / 7 / 10), with fatal errors — e.g. a wrong governing equation or a dimensionally inconsistent formulation — capping the stage score outright.

Crucially, stage scores are then propagated through a dependency graph: a correct downstream step built on a flawed upstream step does not receive full credit, mirroring how errors cascade in real engineering work. Finally, three whole-solution meta-checks — coverage, verbosity, and physical sanity — adjust the aggregate into a single interpretable 0–10 score.

Unlike one-shot judges — which are lenient and overlook mid-solution errors when the final text looks plausible — EngJudge localizes where reasoning breaks down, giving fine-grained diagnostic feedback.

Results

For example, a strong VLM scored an optimistic 8.0 under a one-shot judge but only 2.9 under EngJudge — exposing errors that final-answer grading misses. The same failure profile holds across model families (open and closed, compact and large), indicating the limitation is systematic, not an artifact of model choice.

Human Validation

A blinded study with 9 expert evaluators confirms that EngJudge aligns closely with human expert judgment (Pearson r = 0.975, MAE = 0.67 on a 10-point scale), and experts preferred its dependency-aware scoring over a naive average. Human validation confirms the framework is a reliable tool for process-oriented evaluation.
