Abstract

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) demonstrate strong performance on general multimodal reasoning benchmarks, yet their ability to perform engineering reasoning remains largely unexplored. Unlike general visual question answering, engineering problem solving requires interpreting technical diagrams, selecting governing physical principles, and maintaining physically consistent multi-step reasoning. These capabilities are increasingly important for AI systems used in engineering education, scientific assistance, and technical decision-making, where reasoning failures may produce physically invalid yet superficially plausible solutions.

Existing benchmarks primarily evaluate final answers and provide limited assessment of intermediate reasoning processes. We introduce EngVQA, a multimodal benchmark for evaluating engineering reasoning across 5 engineering subjects containing 696 problems. We introduce EngJudge, an 8-stage automatic evaluation framework for assessing VLM-generated solutions. The framework independently evaluates each stage of the solution, enabling fine-grained analysis of reasoning failures. We benchmark multiple state-of-the-art open and closed source VLMs and demonstrate substantial limitations in current engineering reasoning capabilities. Human evaluation shows strong agreement with our automated framework, achieving a Pearson correlation of 0.975 and a mean absolute error of 0.67 on a 10-point grading scale. Our results highlight the importance of process-oriented evaluation for reliable assessment of multimodal engineering reasoning systems.

Benchmark Design Principles

  1. Diagram-Grounded Analytical Reasoning. Problems require extracting geometry, boundary conditions, force directions, flow structure, material interfaces, and spatial constraints directly from technical figures — free-body diagrams, thermodynamic plots, flow schematics, stress distributions, and engineering layouts.
  2. Structured Multi-Stage Reasoning. Solutions involve multiple interdependent stages: problem characterization, assumption formulation, visual interpretation, equation selection, symbolic derivation, algebraic computation, and physical validation.
  3. Physics-Constrained Engineering Workflows. Problems require physically valid reasoning under domain-specific engineering constraints, maintaining consistency between visual interpretation, governing equations, simplifying assumptions, and final quantitative predictions.

The EngJudge Framework

Evaluating engineering reasoning requires more than final-answer correctness, because failures often emerge within intermediate stages such as assumption formulation, diagram interpretation, equation selection, and computation. EngJudge is a multi-stage, penalty-based automatic framework that decomposes each solution into eight structured reasoning stages and evaluates them individually through an LLM-as-a-judge. It combines penalty-based stage scoring, graph-based dependency propagation, meta-evaluation checks, and exam-style partial credit into a single interpretable score on a 0–10 scale.

The Eight Reasoning Stages

Solutions are generated in a fixed tagged format so they can be parsed automatically:

###### STAGE_NAME ###### [stage content] ###### END_STAGE ######
  1. Problem Characterization — identifies physics domain, problem type, and governing principles.
  2. Assumptions — makes valid, justified assumptions based on problem information.
  3. Visual Interpretation — reads diagrams, FBDs, geometry, and visual boundary conditions.
  4. Equation Selection — selects correct governing equations, BCs, and coordinate systems.
  5. Logical Reasoning — ensures logical validity and physical causality of each step.
  6. Algebraic Accuracy — evaluates derivations, substitutions, and algebraic manipulations.
  7. Physical Interpretation — interprets the physical meaning of the computed result.
  8. Final Answer — compares predicted values with ground truth using numerical error thresholds.

Penalty-Based Stage Scoring

Every stage begins with a score of 10; penalties are deducted for detected errors. The raw stage score is

$$Y_t = \max\!\left(0,\; 10 - \sum_i p_i\right)$$

where $p_i$ is the penalty of the $i$-th identified error in stage $t$, drawn from a four-level severity scale:

SeverityPenaltyExample
Minor2Small rounding differences
Moderate4Unit conversion errors
Major7Wrong boundary conditions
Critical10Dimensional inconsistency

Table 3. The four-level penalty severity scale (2, 4, 7, 10), applied uniformly across all stages. 55.6% of expert evaluators rated this scheme "well calibrated."

Certain critical mistakes cap the maximum stage score regardless of accumulated penalties. The final stage score is the minimum of the additive score and the applicable cap.

Graph-Based Dependency Propagation

Upstream reasoning failures propagate through a dependency DAG that follows the natural flow of an engineering solution. For stage 1 (no parents), $S_1 = Y_1$. Otherwise:

$$S_t = Y_t \times \frac{1}{N_t}\sum_{p \in P(t)} \frac{S_p}{10}$$

where $P(t)$ is the set of parent stages, $N_t = |P(t)|$, and $S_p$ is the propagated score of parent $p$. A correct downstream step built on a flawed upstream step should not receive full credit.

Score Aggregation & Meta-Evaluation Gates

The base score averages the dependency-aware stage scores; missing stages incur a flat penalty; three whole-solution checks apply as multiplicative discounts.

$$S_\text{base} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N} S_t \qquad S_\text{blend} = \max(0,\; S_\text{base} - \text{MP})$$
$$S_\text{final} = S_\text{blend}\cdot(1-\text{COVERAGE})\cdot(1-\text{VERBOSITY})\cdot 0.9^{\text{SANITY\_FAIL}}$$
Coverage. Checks whether all requested quantities, sub-parts, and reasoning steps are addressed. Penalty capped at 0.5.
Verbosity. Penalizes excessive repetition, filler reasoning, and unnecessarily long solutions. Penalty capped at 0.5.
Physical Sanity. Binary check for physically impossible or internally inconsistent outputs; a failure applies a flat 10% (×0.9) penalty.

MP = 2.0 × (number of absent required stages). Coverage and Verbosity are each mapped from a 0–10 evaluator score via $\min(0.5,\,(10-\text{score})\times 0.10)$.

Results

No generator model breaches a mean score of 4 in any subject under EngJudge — highlighting the extreme difficulty of engineering questions for current SOTA VLMs.

Leniency Bias in One-Shot Judging

Under a single-pass judge, Gemini-2.5-Flash receives an optimistic overall score of 8.001. Under the structured, dependency-aware grading of EngJudge, it drops to 2.869. Standard one-shot LLMs suffer from a leniency bias, overlooking mathematical errors, missing justifications, or misinterpreting visuals when the final text appears superficially plausible.

Human Validation Study

A blinded study with 9 expert evaluators (PhD students with TA and grading experience in the chosen subjects) assessed alignment between EngJudge's structured scoring and human judgment. Problems were sampled randomly per evaluator to validate the framework across the widest possible range of domains, reasoning patterns, diagram types, and error modes.

Across 393 rated units (285 stage-level + 108 meta-evaluation), EngJudge aligns strongly with expert judgment. In a blind A/B comparison, evaluators favored the dependency-aware score over a naive average in 66.7% of cases — confirming that engineering evaluation requires hierarchical, cascading penalization rather than isolated stage-wise grading. Additional Experiments & Robustness

Generalization Across Model Families

Beyond Gemini-2.5-Flash and Qwen3-VL-8B, we evaluated InternVL3.5-38B — a larger, different-family open-weight model. Its scores closely follow those of the two original models, and the same stage-wise failure profile is preserved: high scores on setup and problem-characterization stages, collapsing at the downstream execution stages. This indicates the observed limitations are systematic rather than artifacts of model choice.

Robustness of the Agreement

Beyond Pearson $r = 0.975$ and MAE $= 0.67$, the framework achieves a quadratically-weighted Cohen's $\kappa = 0.97$ and a Spearman $\rho = 0.94$ — consistent across linear correlation, rank correlation, and chance-corrected weighted agreement, so the agreement is not an artifact of any single metric.

Generalizability of the Error Taxonomy

Replicating the pilot error analysis with a different pipeline (Qwen3-VL-8B solver + Qwen3-VL-32B judge) reproduces the same dominant failure modes — Equation Selection, Algebraic Accuracy, and Physical Interpretation remain the most frequent error stages (Spearman $\rho = 0.71$). The main model-specific difference is that the smaller Qwen3-VL-8B makes more Visual Interpretation errors (36% vs. 13%), consistent with weaker visual grounding; excluding this expected difference raises agreement to $\rho = 0.89$. The taxonomy captures stable, generalizable reasoning-failure patterns.

No Self-Preference Bias

Cross-evaluation guards against judge bias: if self-preference were driving results, the Gemini judge would favor the Gemini-family generator. Instead its scores are relatively higher for the Qwen generator (~4.0× the Qwen judge) than for Gemini-2.5-Flash (~3.0×), and both judges preserve the same subject ranking.

Representative Failure Modes

Visual Interpretation

Thermodynamics (Prob. 5-131): failed to identify physical stops in a piston-cylinder schematic, modeling the entire compression as isobaric.

Boundary Conditions

Fluid Mechanics (Prob. 11): applied Bernoulli's equation to compute a non-zero exit pressure for an unconfined jet discharging to atmosphere (should be zero gauge pressure).

Dimensionless Groups

Heat & Mass Transfer (Prob. 71): evaluated air properties at free-stream instead of film temperature, corrupting Re and Pr numbers.

Arithmetic & Units

Dynamics (Prob. 21-77): equated spin rate to total angular velocity, ignoring precession — underestimating angular momentum by ~45%.

Computational Cost

EngJudge uses 11 LLM judge calls per solution (8 stage-specific + 3 meta-evaluation). The primary Gemini API run required ~10.8 hours, consuming 36.46M input and 1.77M output tokens (avg. ~58 s and ~55K input tokens per question). The local Qwen pipeline used 11 GPU-hours (6 H100 + 5 L40). The Gemini judge ran at temperature 0.2 with reasoning budgets disabled; Qwen models at temperature 0.7.
